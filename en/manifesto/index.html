Manifesto

Abbia Nòva

An archipelago of land-islands in the municipality of Piglio: a series of small and mid-sized plots, each with its own character, together forming a continuous alternation of cultivation and nature.

We grow vines, olive trees, medicinal herbs, walnuts, and a vegetable garden.

Each island of the archipelago (cru or contrada) is a unit in itself, one with its surroundings and its soil. Over the years, each reveals nuances that define its character; together, they compose a mosaic we have helped build.

We produce wine from four of them (San Giovanni, Cercione, Collepasso, Vignali) and olive oil from five (Costa Ilia, Collepasso, Fontana di Grano, San Giovanni, Mignano).

In some islands, like San Giovanni or Costa Ilia, the plants are very old and have reached an almost total adaptation to the environment.

Medicinal herbs of different species complete the cultivated flora, together with fruit trees and spontaneous herbs. Around each island there are woods, other fields and meadows.

Piglio

Municipality of Piglio, province of Frosinone, forty kilometers south of Rome, Lazio region.

A small enclave with the mountains leading to Abruzzo behind it and the Roman countryside in front. Lazio’s first DOCG.

Bernard of Clairvaux took his vows here (and learned monastic arts, including agriculture) before leaving for Burgundy. Nerva and Trajan would retreat to their summer villas on the fields that Abbia Nòva cultivates today.

The Benedictine Monastery of Santa Scolastica is our agricultural library.

For about four hundred years, one variety has established itself above the others that have passed through the centuries and still try to make themselves noticed in old vineyards.

Cesanese d’Affile plays the leading role in the vineyards, but over time we have also worked to give dignity to other, more ancient grape varieties that we preserve jealously, from Passerina to Nostrano.

Metodo

We come from humble farming families.

Our approach to agriculture is natural and often deeply personal, constantly evolving, yet it reflects us profoundly: a fusion of local traditions and something strongly cultural, built by us through the inspirations and teachings of many natural methods we have studied and experimented with over the years.

Perhaps because our grandparents placed a hoe in our right hand and a metaphorical library in the other, used to deepen different agricultural cultures, treasure them, and merge them with our own. That hoe also represents Piglio’s two-thousand-year winegrowing tradition, on volcanic soils, with meticulous care of the plants, with vineyards that have always shared space with gardens, fruit trees, and animals.

Our cellar is a small isolated farmhouse in the mountains, along the Roman consular road that once connected Lazio to Abruzzo: two rooms where amphorae, concrete, steel, wood and glass demijohns help us. The only tools, coordinated by the mind and the hands.
